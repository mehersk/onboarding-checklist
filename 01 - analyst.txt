  ---
  Product Brief: Developer Onboarding Checklist App

  Executive Summary

  Engineering onboarding at scale is broken. When a new developer joins a team,
  they face a chaotic first week: Confluence pages nobody updates, Slack threads
   asking what to set up next, and a Team Lead who cannot track progress without
   scheduling yet another check-in call. The result is slow ramp-up times,
  frustrated new hires, and avoidable attrition.

  The Developer Onboarding Checklist App is an internal web tool that brings
  structure, visibility, and accountability to the first 30–90 days of every new
   engineer's journey. New joiners follow a role-specific checklist — tailored
  for Frontend, Backend, DevOps, or QA engineers — with milestone-based
  sign-offs from their Team Lead at each weekly phase boundary. HR manages
  checklist templates independently, without requiring an engineering ticket.
  Every stakeholder gets exactly the visibility they need, and nothing more.

  For a 50–100 person engineering organisation, this is not a nice-to-have. It
  is the foundation for consistent, measurable ramp-up — and the prerequisite
  for confident hiring growth.

  The Problem

  Today, engineering onboarding relies on a patchwork of Confluence wikis,
  Notion docs, and word-of-mouth guidance. The consequences are predictable and
  costly:

  - New joiners don't know what "done" looks like. Without a structured task
  list, they default to asking their Team Lead or posting in Slack: "What should
   I be doing this week?" These questions are answered inconsistently and burn
  senior engineer time.
  - Team Leads cannot see progress without asking. The only way to know whether
  a joiner has completed their Week 1 tasks is to schedule a check-in call —
  synchronous overhead that neither party needs.
  - HR cannot update onboarding materials without filing a ticket. When the
  checklist needs to change, HR must ask engineering, introducing delays and
  version drift.
  - There is no role-specific standard. A Frontend hire and a DevOps hire follow
   the same generic process, even though their setup, tooling, and first-week
  tasks are fundamentally different.

  The measurable impact: time-to-first-PR currently exceeds 5 days.

  The Solution

  A self-hosted internal web application, authenticated via Google Workspace or
  Azure AD SSO:

  New joiners see a role-specific checklist (Frontend / Backend / DevOps / QA)
  with a ramp period matched to seniority: 30 days for juniors, 60 or 90 days
  for senior and staff engineers. Tasks are grouped into weekly phases.
  Automated reminders surface overdue items before they become blockers.

  Team Leads have a real-time progress view for each direct report. At the end
  of each phase (Week 1, Week 2, final completion), they provide a milestone
  sign-off that formally advances the joiner's record.

  HR / People Ops manages all templates through a self-service interface.
  Templates are versioned — updates apply to new joiners only; in-progress
  onboarding is unaffected.

  What Makes This Different

  - Role-typed by default. Four engineering tracks are first-class, not
  afterthoughts inside a generic HR tool.
  - Milestone sign-off, not per-task approval. Team Leads interact at natural
  phase boundaries — matching how engineers think about progress.
  - HR template ownership without engineering dependency. A structural design
  principle, not a feature.
  - Zero disruption to in-progress joiners. Snapshot-at-assignment versioning.
  - Right-sized for the organisation. Deliberately simple, fast to adopt,
  operates behind a corporate firewall.

  Who This Serves

  New Joiners (primary) — Engineers in their first 30–90 days. Their aha moment
  is Day 1: a checklist built for their role, not a wall of generic wiki links.

  Team Leads (primary) — Engineering managers who open a dashboard instead of
  scheduling a call. Milestone sign-off at each phase boundary.

  HR / People Ops (secondary) — Edit a template directly, publish it
  immediately, know in-progress joiners are unaffected.

  Success Criteria

  1. Time-to-first-PR drops below 5 days — measured from start date to first
  merged PR, per role and seniority band.
  2. "What do I do next?" Slack messages to HR drop to near zero in Week 1 —
  confirmed with the HR team within 60 days of launch.
  3. Team Leads view joiner progress without scheduling a check-in call —
  telemetry confirms dashboard views replace the check-in cadence.

  Scope

  v1 includes: Role-specific checklists (4 roles) · Configurable ramp period
  (30/60/90 days) · Weekly phase grouping with milestone sign-off · HR
  self-service templates with version-safe publishing · Team Lead progress
  dashboard · SSO (Google Workspace / Azure AD) · Email or Slack reminders for
  overdue tasks · Self-hosted deployment behind corporate firewall

  Deferred to v2: GitHub / Jira integration · HR/leadership analytics dashboard
  · Mobile experience · Automated access provisioning

  Roadmap Thinking

  If v1 succeeds, v2 direction is integration depth: GitHub PR merges confirming
   task completion, Slack notifications for milestone sign-offs, and a reporting
   layer for cohort ramp time tracking. In two to three years, this becomes the
  authoritative record of engineering onboarding at the organisation — a
  structured, queryable history of every hire's first 90 days. v1 earns that
  future by being the tool people actually use.
