  🏗️ Winston:

  Let me lay out what I'm actually seeing here, because I think the
  classification is doing some work it isn't equipped for.

  On "Medium" complexity — I'd push this to High.

  Look at what's actually on the table:

  - SSO integration (Google Workspace or Azure AD — that's two auth paths, not
  one)
  - Template versioning with snapshot-at-assignment semantics — that's an audit
  trail problem, not a CRUD problem. You're preserving historical state
  per-assignment, which means your data model has real temporal complexity baked
   in from day one
  - Milestone sign-off with automatic escalation on a 5-business-day timer —
  business-day calendaring, not wall-clock time, which means you need holiday
  awareness or you'll have escalations firing on Christmas
  - Multi-channel notifications (email and Slack) with retry semantics
  - Four distinct roles with meaningfully different UX surfaces
  - Pulse scoring per phase — simple on the surface, but it creates reporting
  expectations downstream

  Any one of these is fine. All of them together, on a greenfield system, with a
   self-hosted constraint? That's High. The failure mode of calling this Medium
  is that you scope the initial build for Medium, hit the auth integration in
  week two, and suddenly the timeline looks wrong and everyone's surprised.

  On "web_app" vs. a saas_b2b-style internal platform:

  Mechanically, "self-hosted internal tool" sounds like web_app. But look at
  what you're building: multi-role model, lifecycle state machines per user,
  templating engine with versioning, scheduled background jobs, notification
  pipelines, reporting aggregation. The shape of this system is closer to B2B
  SaaS than a typical internal CRUD app.

  I wouldn't rename to saas_b2b — that carries connotations (billing,
  provisioning) that don't apply. But I would call this a workflow platform, not
   a checklist app. The escalation logic, versioned templates, audit semantics —
   those are workflow engine concerns.

  What I'd change:

  1. Complexity: High, not Medium. Be honest about what's here.
  2. Keep web_app for the label, but explicitly call out background job system
  and notification pipeline as first-class architectural concerns, not features.
  3. Add a constraint: self-hosted behind a firewall means no managed services
  for the scheduler or notification pipeline — that's in scope from architecture
   day one, not discovered at deployment.

  📊 Mary:

  Oh, this is the clue I've been waiting for someone to pull on. "General" is
  doing a lot of quiet work here, and I think it's hiding things we need to
  surface.

  What "general" is papering over:

  This application touches three categories of data that have real compliance
  gravity:

  1. Employment records. Onboarding progress, milestone sign-off timestamps,
  ramp period assignments — these are HR artifacts. Under GDPR, CCPA, and
  employment law frameworks, records documenting an employee's progress during
  onboarding are personnel records. Employees may have rights to access,
  correct, or request deletion of them.
  2. Audit trails. The milestone sign-off feature and escalation logic are,
  functionally, a lightweight performance-management record system. Who signed
  off what, when, and whether it escalated — that's exactly the kind of data
  that surfaces in employment disputes, termination proceedings, and labour
  audits. Once you have escalation logic, you have a paper trail with legal
  weight.
  3. Pulse scores. A 1–5 self-reported sentiment score tied to an identified
  employee is sensitive HR data. It's not anonymised, not aggregate-only — it's
  "Joiner X reported feeling a 2/5 at the 30-day mark." That lives in a
  personnel file if anyone subpoenas it.

  What I'd reclassify this as:

  The most precise domain label is hr-tech / people-ops, with a data privacy
  compliance overlay. This changes the PRD requirements in concrete ways:

  - Data retention policies must be specified: how long do we keep completed
  onboarding records, who can delete them, and can they be hard-deleted or only
  soft-deleted?
  - Access control needs to be tighter than "roles" — does a Team Lead retain
  access to a joiner's pulse scores after the joiner changes teams?
  - Data export / right-of-access: if a former employee asks what data you hold,
   what does the system produce?
  - Audit log integrity: the escalation trail should not be editable by admins.

  🎨 Sally:

  Let me paint the scene, because I think we're underselling the complexity and
  that's going to bite us.

  Imagine four people opening the same URL, having four completely different
  emotional experiences. The new joiner is anxious — day two, doesn't know what
  they don't know, needs a single calming answer to "am I doing okay?" The Team
  Lead opens the app between back-to-back meetings and needs a dashboard
  communicating status in eight seconds. HR is in there on a Tuesday afternoon
  authoring a template, thinking in policies and milestones, not Jira tickets.
  The Engineering Manager gets a notification, clicks in, and needs to act —
  approve, escalate, reassign — without context-switching into a whole new
  mental model.

  That is not one product. That is four products sharing a database.

  On "web_app" vs. internal platform:

  "Web app" suggests a relatively unified surface — one primary user type with a
   couple of admin screens bolted on. What we actually have is a
  role-differentiated platform with distinct navigation architectures,
  information hierarchies, and interaction patterns per persona. The closest
  analogy isn't a checklist app — it's closer to Greenhouse or Notion, where the
   same URL serves radically different mental models. I'd reclassify this as an
  internal role-based platform. The classification matters because it changes IA
   decisions from day one.

  On "medium" complexity:

  Medium assumes a relatively stable user mental model across the app. We don't
  have that. We have four mental models, four definitions of "done," four
  anxiety profiles. The multi-persona design challenge alone — a single workflow
   (completing a checklist item) has to surface correctly to the New Joiner,
  notify the Team Lead, and potentially escalate to the EM — that's not medium.
  I'd call this high UX complexity, even if engineering complexity stays
  medium-ish.

  Signals we're missing:

  1. Permission and visibility architecture — who sees whose data, in what
  state, is a design problem before it's an engineering problem
  2. The non-technical author problem — HR authoring templates is a product
  design challenge in itself; template versioning for someone who thinks in Word
   documents, not CMSes
  3. Emotional state design — the New Joiner's experience has to be warm and
  orienting, not clinical; you can't design one neutral UI for all four roles
  4. The escalation path UX — EM as escalation recipient is a distinct
  notification-and-action design surface

  Reclassify as a role-based internal platform at high UX complexity. Those are
  fundamentally different design strategies, and making that choice now saves a
  very painful redesign at 60% through build.

  We've established this is a web_app in the hr-tech domain with High complexity
   — a role-based workflow platform where four distinct mental models share a
  database. Now let's get to the soul of it.

  know. Here's what I'm already holding as your vision foundation:

  What I hear as the core insight: The patchwork of Confluence wikis and Slack

   onboarding doesn't exist yet at the engineering layer. The real problem isn't
   "no checklist" — it's that accountability, clarity, and visibility are all
  missing simultaneously across three different stakeholders who each have a
  different relationship to the same onboarding event.

  What I hear as the differentiator: Everything else in this space is either too
   generic (BambooHR, Workday — HR tools that don't know what a Backend engineer
   needs on Day 1) or too heavy (Backstage — requires a dedicated platform
  team). You're filling the specific gap between those: purpose-built for
  engineering roles, lightweight enough for a 50–100 person org to actually
  adopt, and structured around the three-way relationship between joiner, Team
  Lead, and HR rather than just the joiner's experience in isolation.

  What I hear as the future state: A Team Lead opens a dashboard instead of
  scheduling a check-in call. A new joiner knows exactly what "done" looks like.
   HR publishes a template update on a Tuesday without opening a ticket. And
  time-to-first-PR drops below 5 days — not because the code got easier, but
  because nobody is blocked by missing information.

  3. The vision horizon: The brief talks about v1 earning the right to v2. What

  ---
  Executive Summary

  The Developer Onboarding Checklist App is an internal, self-hosted web
  platform that replaces ad-hoc engineering onboarding — Confluence wikis, Slack
   questions, manual check-in calls — with a structured, role-aware, accountable
   system for the first 30–90 days of every new engineer's journey.

  Context: A senior backend engineer joined last quarter and submitted their
  first PR on day 11, not day 5 — a week spent chasing access credentials and
  asking Slack questions that nobody owned. The org is about to hire 15
  engineers across two quarters; the next cohort starts in 8 weeks. Broken
  onboarding at 50-person scale is painful. At 100-person scale, it compounds.

  The core problem is not missing checklists — it is missing accountability 
  across three relationships simultaneously:
  - New joiners lack clarity on what "done" looks like at any phase boundary
  - Team Leads have no visibility into joiner progress without scheduling a
  synchronous check-in
  - HR cannot update onboarding content without filing an engineering ticket

  Target users: New Joiners (primary — engineers in their first 30–90 days);
  Team Leads (primary — engineering managers and senior leads responsible for
  ramp); HR/People Ops (co-primary — own the template content surface);
  Engineering Managers (escalation path when Team Lead sign-off lapses beyond 5
  business days).

  North star: No new joiner should ever have to ask "what am I supposed to be 
  doing right now?" — and no Team Lead should have to schedule a meeting to find
   out if their joiner is okay.

  The vision at 6 months: Onboarding is boring. New joiners are heads-down and
  productive. Team Leads stopped scheduling weekly check-ins because the
  dashboard already told them. HR publishes template updates that nobody notices
   because they just work. The org has forgotten what the old way felt like.

  Design principle — the tool must feel invisible to the joiner. It must not
  feel like compliance software or a ticketing system. If it feels like
  paperwork, adoption dies. Every UX decision is filtered through this
  constraint: the joiner's surface should feel like a personal task list, not an
   HR form.

  ---
  What Makes This Special

  Not a generic task manager. Not a developer portal. Not a wiki. Three things
  together — in a single lightweight, self-hosted tool — that no current
  solution delivers:

  1. Role-typed by default. Five engineering tracks (Frontend, Backend, DevOps,
  QA, Custom) are first-class, not configured-in afterthoughts. A Backend joiner
   and a DevOps joiner get fundamentally different checklists from Day 1 — no
  forcing anyone into a template that's 60% relevant.
  2. Structured accountability without overhead. Milestone sign-off at phase
  boundaries — not per-task approvals — gives Team Leads a structured touchpoint
   at the natural rhythm of engineering progress. Automatic escalation to
  Engineering Manager after 5 business days removes the need for manual
  intervention when a Team Lead is unavailable. Joiners can flag blocked tasks
  in-product with a one-line reason — keeping friction inside the system rather
  than leaking back to Slack.
  3. HR template ownership without engineering dependency. HR can draft,
  version, and publish templates without opening a ticket.
  Snapshot-at-assignment versioning ensures in-progress joiners are never
  disrupted by a template update. The org/content boundary is structurally
  enforced, not a convention that relies on discipline.

  Why not the alternatives: BambooHR and Workday don't know what a backend
  engineer needs on Day 1. Backstage requires a dedicated platform team to
  maintain. Notion and Confluence lack enforced workflow, sign-off
  accountability, and audit semantics. This fills the gap between HR tooling and
   developer portals — purpose-built for engineering roles, lightweight enough
  for a 50–100 person org to actually adopt.

  Self-hosted architectural forcing function: All background scheduling (overdue
   reminders, escalation timers) and notification pipelines (email, Slack) are
  first-class system design concerns — no managed services available behind the
  corporate firewall. This is not a deployment detail; it shapes the
  architecture from day one.

  User Success

  The product succeeds for users when:

  - New joiners never ask "what am I supposed to be doing right now." Day 1

  criterion is failing.
  - Joiners feel unblocked at end of Week 1. Captured via the milestone pulse
  score (1–5). Target: average ≥4/5 across the first three hiring cohorts using
  the tool. A joiner who is blocked can flag the specific task in-product with a
   one-line reason — no Slack message required.
  - Team Leads get progress visibility without scheduling a call. Opening the
  dashboard replaces the weekly "how's it going?" check-in. The pulse score
  surfaces early-warning signals before they require a conversation.
  - HR publishes a template update and nobody notices. Snapshot-at-assignment
  versioning means the update silently applies to new joiners only. In-progress
  joiners are unaffected. No tickets, no coordination overhead.

  - Self-hosted deployment is clean behind the corporate firewall — no external

  - Background scheduler is reliable — overdue reminders fire at the correct

  5 business days, not wall-clock days; holiday awareness implemented
  - Notification pipeline delivers with retry semantics — email and Slack
  reminders reach recipients; failed deliveries are logged and retried; no
  silent failures
  - Audit log integrity is maintained — milestone sign-off records are immutable
   and timestamped; escalation trail is not editable by any role including
  admins
  - Template versioning is correct — zero in-progress joiners are disrupted by
  any template update; snapshot-at-assignment semantics hold under concurrent
  updates
  - Data retention is implemented — onboarding records and pulse scores are
  soft-deleted (not hard-deleted); configurable retention period; data export
  available for right-of-access requests (GDPR/CCPA compliance)

  3. HR confirms "what do I do next?" messages have materially dropped
  4. ≥80% of Team Leads report no dedicated onboarding check-in call in their

  Product Scope

  MVP — Minimum Viable Product

  Everything required for the tool to be the single authoritative onboarding
  channel from day one of the next cohort (8 weeks):

  - Role-specific checklists: Frontend, Backend, DevOps, QA, Custom (5 tracks)
  - Configurable ramp period: 30 / 60 / 90 days by seniority
  - Weekly phase grouping with milestone sign-off (Week 1, Week 2, final
  completion)
  - Automatic sign-off escalation to Engineering Manager after 5 business days
  (business-day aware)
  - Joiner "flag as blocked" per task — flag icon + one-line free-text reason
  - Milestone pulse score — one question per sign-off ("How is your onboarding
  going? 1–5"), visible on Team Lead dashboard
  - HR self-service template management with version-safe publishing
  (snapshot-at-assignment)
  - Team Lead progress dashboard — per-joiner, per-phase, including pulse scores
  - SSO authentication — Google Workspace and Azure AD
  - Email and Slack reminders for overdue tasks (with retry semantics)
  - Self-hosted deployment behind corporate firewall
  - Soft-delete with audit log integrity; basic data export for GDPR/CCPA
  compliance
  - Pre-launch gate (not a product feature): Joint engineering + HR content
  session to author initial task lists for all 5 role tracks

  Growth Features (Post-MVP)

  Features that make the tool competitive and extend its value after v1 proves
  adoption:

  - GitHub PR merge events confirming task completion automatically
  - Jira ticket integration for access-provisioning tasks
  - HR and leadership cohort analytics dashboard (ramp time trends, pulse
  trends)
  - Slack-native notifications for milestone sign-offs (beyond current
  reminder-only model)
  - Mobile web experience

  The authoritative record of engineering onboarding at the organisation:

  - Structured, queryable history of every hire's first 90 days — including
  weekly pulse scores — feeding into team health metrics and performance review
  context
  - Cohort comparison: ramp time and experience quality across quarters and
  hiring waves
  - Automated access provisioning tied to checklist task completion (IAM
  integration)
  - Onboarding quality as a metric in engineering leadership OKRs
